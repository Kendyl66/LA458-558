Iowa Territory

July 4, 1838

Iowa Territory was incorporated into the United States in 1838. While slavery was illegal in Iowa, the territorial legislature was dominated by individuals from southern states, whose views were mirrored those devised in the South.


Black Laws

1839

The Laws of the Territory stated that African-Americans who desired to live in Iowa had to “register with the clerk’s office in each county and post a five hundred dollar bond, guaranteeing their good behavior and preventing their becoming public charges of the state.” These were the start of Iowa’s black codes which determined the fate of any African-American arriving across its borders.


July 4, 1839

The "Ralph"
Case

In 1830, Ralph, an African American born a slave in Virginia, was sold to Jordan J. Montgomery as a field hand and moved with him to Marion County, Missouri. In 1834, Ralph was provided a written contract where Montgomery permitted him to leave Missouri and move to the future Iowa Territory with the agreement that Ralph would pay him back $500 dollars for his hire and interest (Silag et al., 2001). However, in 1839, Montgomery sent people to Dubuque where Ralph had been mining for lead, with the intention of selling him for a profit. Ralph was taken into custody and put on a boat to Missouri, but a few of his fellow miners “sought a writ of habeas corpus on Ralph’s behalf” from a member of the Iowa Territorial Supreme Court and were able to get him off the boat and in front of a judge (Silag et al., 2001). Ultimately, the Iowa Territorial Supreme Court heard the case and on the same day ordered that “Ralph a man of color is free by operation of law” (Supreme Court Order Book, vol. A (1838-53), 6 quoted in Outside In, 63).


1841

Updated Black Laws

The legislature added onto the black laws by passing law which stated that interracial marriage was prohibited. During this time, there were other constitutional restrictions placed into the black laws barring anyone from but white males to vote and fight in the militia, and excluded persons considered “negro, mulatto or Indian” from testifying “in any court or any case against a white person.” It was also stated that slaveholders visiting the state could bring their slaves only for a short period of time.


Statehood

December 28, 1846

President James K. Polk made Iowa the 29th State in the United States. Originally, the size of Iowa was much larger, pushing up into Minnesota and the Dakotas, however, representatives from the norther states desired smaller boarders for Iowa in order to leave more land to develop “free” states for freed or runaway slaves to reside in. They all finally agreed on the current size and shape of Iowa.


1840s-1850s

Before Fugitive Slave Act

Slaves who sought refuge in Iowa did not generally have a smooth transition into free land. Years before the Fugitive Slave Act came into effect, “slave owners from Missouri and Nebraska came to Iowa to reclaim slaves” and in places such as Keokuk, southern Iowans became slave catchers who then returned runaway slaves back to their owners. Despite this, slaves kept taking refuge in Iowa and risking their lives to be free.

Underground Railroad

1850s

Des Moines was part of the Underground Railroad system which provided a network for slaves to flee the south and cross into free states, such as Iowa. It is noted that an abolitionist by the name of John Brown “escorted a group through the city in 1858” while others housed the slaves as they made their journey to Canada(Lufkin, 17).


Iowa's Exclusionary Law

1851

Enactment of the 1851 Exclusionary Law in Iowa. (link to federal Housing page) This law, however, was not really enforced and in 1864 it was repealed toward the end of the Civil War.

Iowa Coal Industry

1880s

Starting in the 1880s, Iowa’s coal industry boomed brining in a large black population to work in the mines across the entire state. Instead of being concentrated to a few bigger cities, they were spread across the entire state. An increase of close to 4,000 African-Americans between 1870-80.

Iowa State Bystander

1894

The Iowa State Bystander, an African-American newspaper in the early 20th century, was created and it was a display of the pride and ethic awareness among the African-American community in Des Moines.


1900

Diverse Iowa

More than 1,600 African-Americans made up close to 3% of the city’s population which was a high percentage compared to other midwestern cities and by 1915, those numbers rose to 3.8% (4,062).

1914-1918

World War I

During World War 1 there was a scarcity of white labor Iowa was very receptive to the influx of black migrants from the South where they experienced unprecedented job opportunities in the industries within Iowa. Many blacks moved “steadily from the predominant menial and janitorial occupations into the ranks of semi-skilled and skilled labor”.

Fort Des Moines

1917

First training corps for black officers in the United States was at Fort Des Moines, which increased migration and because of the war, this greatly impacted the number of jobs available for African Americans. Between 1910-1920, the Black Population saw a 29% increase.

1920

World War I: The Aftermath

In Des Moines, The African-American population hit its first peak with a 29% increase from 1910; 14,972 to 19,005. After the war, when white soldiers returned, African-Americans “in Iowa, as in other states, found themselves in intense competition with whites for jobs.” They lost economic and occupation progress, and when the Great depression hit, the black population was hit very hard with a 50% unemployment rate and a lack of migration to the state. They were also some of the first groups to experience the initial stages leading up to the Great Depression.

1930s-1940s

The Great Depression

Between the two World Wars, Iowa lost “thousands of African-Americans due to economic hardship” while the rest of the state’s white population increased by 66,000. In 1930, only about one half of the around 14,500 African-Americans living in the largest cities in Iowa were employed and about one fourth of the employed men worked in domestic services.

1945

The GI Bill

The GI Bill and the Veterans Administration provided housing loans and financial assistance to soldiers adjusting back to life after World War II. However, these benefits were not accessible to African American soldiers.

1950s

Post-War African American Population

After the Great Depression and World War II, in the 1950s, the African-American population in Iowa increased to around 19,692 or 0.8% of the total population in Iowa.

White Flight

1950s-70s

The movement of white families and residents from urban areas with predominately minority populations to suburban areas. This rose from fear facilitated by the FHA and lenders about decreasing property values in mixed neighborhoods.

Urban Renewal

1950s-70s

The rise in urban renewal projects across the entire came to Des Moines around 1957, when discussions about new highways and the clearing of "blighted" areas for new developments arose. In Des Moines, the urban renewal process worked its way through predominately low-income and African-American neighborhoods who were treated without much respect in terms of good communication and aid in finding new housing.

Good Park Riots

July 1966

Good Park (1155 17th street, Des Moines, IA 50314) was the main hang out for kids and teenagers since the early 1940s. there was a wading pool, shelter, basketball courts and lots of green space to play. When the riot happened in Good Park in July 1966, some of the young people involved told the Des Moines Register reporters that “there was not enough for them to do at night. Some complained of the inability to find jobs; others expressed concern about the rough police treatment.” The riots occurred “at a time when perceptions of police brutality were sparking riots in many American cities. African Americans, many stuck in northern urban ghettos with few economic opportunities, grew impatient with the slow progress of the mainstream civil rights movement.”

The Black Panther Party

July 1968

The Black Panther party emerged in Des Moines in 1968. The BPP grew quickly in Des Moines so the Oakland based organization did not have the capacity to exert any type of control over them.


1988

Iowa Hate Crimes Act

In 1988, Iowa passed the Iowa Hate Crimes Act which emphasized the need to educate and challenge people who were practicing racially motivated hate and violence. When it passed it committed anyone who participated of a felony or misdemeanor and was expanded in 1990 to expand the classes of who was protected under it.

Present Day

2010

Looking at housing explicitly, in 2010, 65% of cost burdened renters in Des Moines were African American, they represented only 2.6% of all homeowners in the entire county, and in the tracts where many minorities lived had homes with a median value of $70,000 compared to the Polk County median value of $140,6000 (half). There were 4,600 households waiting for Section 8 vouchers.
